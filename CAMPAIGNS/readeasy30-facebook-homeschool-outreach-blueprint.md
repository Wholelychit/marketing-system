- free reading practice

## Safety rule before posting

Read group rules first.

Do not post if the group bans links, self-promotion, resources, or outside sites.

Better approach:

1. Comment helpfully first.
2. Share the site only when relevant.
3. Ask for feedback instead of acting like a finished authority.
4. Avoid medical claims.
5. Avoid saying it is designed specifically for ADHD, autism, dyslexia, or special education unless the site has reviewed support language.

## Facebook post blueprint

I noticed that many online educational sites can feel too busy for some learners: too many pop-ups, flashing elements, noisy screens, or pressure to move fast.

ReadEasy30 is meant to be calmer. It uses short reading lessons, simple questions, read-aloud support, and a gentle bubble mascot named Bubbles who encourages learners to slow down and look for proof in the text.

I am also building printable reading worksheet content for families who prefer paper practice instead of more screen time.

The goal is a simple 30-minute daily reading routine with less stress and more confidence.

It may be useful for:

- homeschool families
- parents helping struggling readers
- tutors and helpers
- ESL learners
- adult learners who want private practice
- learners who do better with a calmer practice screen

ReadEasy30 is a practice resource, not a replacement for teachers, tutors, specialists, or school support.

## Comment reply templates

### If someone asks if it is really free

The goal is to keep it easy to start. Right now it is built as a simple reading-practice resource without requiring a complicated setup.

### If someone asks if it is for special education

It may be useful for some learners who need calmer practice, but I avoid calling it a special education replacement. It is a practice resource, not a school plan, diagnosis tool, or therapy program.

### If someone asks if it is for dyslexia or ADHD

It is not a treatment or diagnostic tool. The design goal is to reduce pressure, clutter, and guessing by using short lessons, simple questions, and a calm reading routine.

### If someone says their child hates reading

That is common. I would keep practice short, read together first, praise effort, and stop before frustration wins. The goal is to rebuild confidence slowly.

### If someone asks about printables

Printable reading worksheet content is part of the plan because some learners do better with paper than another screen. The goal is to keep it simple and practical.
